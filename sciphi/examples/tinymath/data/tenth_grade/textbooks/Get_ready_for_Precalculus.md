### Section: Quadratic Equations with Complex Solutions

In the previous section, we explored the operations of adding and subtracting complex numbers. Now, let's move on to another important topic in precalculus: quadratic equations with complex solutions.

A quadratic equation is an equation of the form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$, where $a$, $b$, and $c$ are constants and $x$ is the variable. The solutions to a quadratic equation are the values of $x$ that make the equation true.

Quadratic equations can have different types of solutions. In some cases, the solutions are real numbers, meaning they can be found on the real number line. However, there are also cases where the solutions are complex numbers, which cannot be found on the real number line.

Complex numbers are numbers of the form $a + bi$, where $a$ and $b$ are real numbers and $i$ is the imaginary unit. The imaginary unit $i$ is defined as $\sqrt{-1}$, and it allows us to work with numbers that involve the square root of a negative number.

To solve a quadratic equation with complex solutions, we can use the quadratic formula:

$$
x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}
$$

In this formula, $a$, $b$, and $c$ are the coefficients of the quadratic equation. The $\pm$ symbol indicates that there are two possible solutions, one with a plus sign and one with a minus sign.

Let's consider an example to illustrate how to solve a quadratic equation with complex solutions. Suppose we have the equation $x^2 + 4x + 5 = 0$. We can identify the coefficients as $a = 1$, $b = 4$, and $c = 5$. Plugging these values into the quadratic formula, we get:

$$
x = \frac{-4 \pm \sqrt{4^2 - 4(1)(5)}}{2(1)}
$$

Simplifying further, we have:

$$
x = \frac{-4 \pm \sqrt{16 - 20}}{2}
$$

$$
x = \frac{-4 \pm \sqrt{-4}}{2}
$$

Since the square root of a negative number is not a real number, we can rewrite $\sqrt{-4}$ as $2i$. Therefore, the solutions to the equation are:

$$
x = \frac{-4 + 2i}{2} \quad \text{and} \quad x = \frac{-4 - 2i}{2}
$$

Simplifying these expressions, we find that the solutions are:

$$
x = -2 + i \quad \text{and} \quad x = -2 - i
$$

These solutions are complex numbers, as they involve the imaginary unit $i$. Quadratic equations with complex solutions often arise in various fields of mathematics and science, such as engineering and physics.

$$
f(x - 3) = (x - 3)^2

#### Definition of Inverse Functions

To put it simply, if $f(a) = b$, then $f^{-1}(b) = a$. The inverse function reverses the mapping of the original function.

#### Finding the Inverse Function

To find the inverse function of a given function, we follow a specific process. Let's consider a function $f(x)$:

1. Replace $f(x)$ with $y$. This gives us the equation $y = f(x)$.

3. Solve the equation for $y$. This will give us the inverse function $f^{-1}(y)$.

It's important to note that not all functions have an inverse. For a function to have an inverse, it must satisfy certain conditions. One of these conditions is that the function must be one-to-one, meaning that each input corresponds to a unique output and vice versa.

#### Definition and properties

Radians are a way to measure angles based on the radius of a circle. Unlike degrees, which divide a circle into 360 equal parts, radians divide a circle into $2\pi$ equal parts. One radian is defined as the angle subtended at the center of a circle by an arc that is equal in length to the radius of the circle.

To better understand this, let's consider a circle with a radius of 1 unit, also known as the unit circle. If we were to travel along the circumference of the unit circle for a distance equal to its radius, we would have traveled one radian.

The use of radians is particularly advantageous when working with trigonometric functions because it simplifies calculations and allows for more precise measurements. In fact, many trigonometric identities and formulas are derived using radians.

Here are some important properties of radians:

1. Conversion between degrees and radians:
   - To convert from degrees to radians, multiply the degree measure by $\frac{\pi}{180}$.
   - To convert from radians to degrees, multiply the radian measure by $\frac{180}{\pi}$.

2. Radian measure of a full circle:
   - A full circle measures $2\pi$ radians. This is equivalent to 360 degrees.

3. Relationship between arc length, radius, and angle:
   - The length of an arc on the unit circle is equal to the radian measure of the corresponding angle.
   - For example, an angle of $\frac{\pi}{2}$ radians corresponds to an arc length of $\frac{\pi}{2}$ units on the unit circle.

4. Trigonometric functions and radians:
   - Trigonometric functions, such as sine, cosine, and tangent, are defined using radians.
   - When using these functions, the input angle is typically given in radians.

Understanding radians and their properties is crucial for mastering trigonometry. It allows us to work seamlessly with the unit circle and simplifies calculations involving trigonometric functions.

### Section: The Pythagorean Identity

In the previous section, we explored the concept of radians and their significance in trigonometry. Now, let's delve into another important concept known as the Pythagorean Identity. This identity is a fundamental equation that relates the three main trigonometric functions: sine, cosine, and tangent.

#### Definition of the Pythagorean Identity

The Pythagorean Identity states that for any angle $θ$ in a right triangle, the following equation holds true:

$$\sin^2θ + \cos^2θ = 1$$

This equation may look familiar to you, as it closely resembles the famous Pythagorean Theorem. Just like the Pythagorean Theorem relates the lengths of the sides of a right triangle, the Pythagorean Identity relates the values of the trigonometric functions for a given angle.

#### Derivation of the Pythagorean Identity

To understand how the Pythagorean Identity is derived, let's consider a right triangle with an angle $θ$. We can label the sides of the triangle as follows:

- The side opposite to the angle $θ$ is called the **opposite side** and is denoted as $a$.
- The side adjacent to the angle $θ$ is called the **adjacent side** and is denoted as $b$.
- The hypotenuse, which is the side opposite the right angle, is denoted as $c$.

Using the definitions of sine and cosine, we can express the lengths of the sides in terms of $θ$:

$$\sinθ = \frac{a}{c}$$

$$\cosθ = \frac{b}{c}$$

Now, let's square both sides of these equations:

$$(\sinθ)^2 = \left(\frac{a}{c}\right)^2$$

$$(\cosθ)^2 = \left(\frac{b}{c}\right)^2$$

Next, let's add these two equations together:

$$(\sinθ)^2 + (\cosθ)^2 = \left(\frac{a}{c}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{b}{c}\right)^2$$

Using the fact that $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ (which is the Pythagorean Theorem), we can simplify the equation:

$$(\sinθ)^2 + (\cosθ)^2 = \frac{a^2}{c^2} + \frac{b^2}{c^2}$$

$$(\sinθ)^2 + (\cosθ)^2 = \frac{a^2 + b^2}{c^2}$$

Since $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$, we can substitute this into the equation:

$$(\sinθ)^2 + (\cosθ)^2 = \frac{c^2}{c^2}$$

$$(\sinθ)^2 + (\cosθ)^2 = 1$$

And there we have it! The Pythagorean Identity is derived by using the definitions of sine and cosine and the Pythagorean Theorem.

#### Importance of the Pythagorean Identity

The Pythagorean Identity is a crucial tool in trigonometry. It allows us to relate the values of sine and cosine for any given angle. This relationship is particularly useful when solving trigonometric equations and identities.

By manipulating the Pythagorean Identity, we can derive other important trigonometric identities. These identities help simplify trigonometric expressions and solve various types of trigonometric equations.

#### Equivalent Systems of Equations

Two systems of equations are said to be equivalent if they have the same solution set. In other words, if two systems of equations represent the same relationships between variables, they will have the same solutions.

To determine if two systems of equations are equivalent, we can compare their equations and coefficients. If the equations are the same or can be obtained from each other by performing the same operations, then the systems are equivalent.

#### The Elimination Method

The elimination method, also known as the addition method or the method of linear combination, is a systematic approach to solving systems of equations. It involves eliminating one variable at a time by adding or subtracting the equations in the system.

Here are the steps to solve a system of equations using the elimination method:

1. Write the system of equations in standard form, with the variables aligned vertically.
2. Choose one variable to eliminate first. Look for a pair of equations where the coefficients of that variable have opposite signs or are multiples of each other.
3. Multiply one or both equations by appropriate constants so that the coefficients of the chosen variable have opposite signs or are multiples of each other.
4. Add or subtract the equations to eliminate the chosen variable. This will result in a new equation with one less variable.
5. Solve the new equation for the remaining variable.
6. Substitute the value of the remaining variable back into one of the original equations to find the value of the eliminated variable.
7. Check the solution by substituting the values of the variables into all the original equations. The solution should satisfy all the equations.

Let's look at an example to illustrate the elimination method:

Example:
Solve the following system of equations using the elimination method:

$$
\begin{align*}
2x + 3y &= 7 \\
4x - 2y &= 2 \\
\end{align*}
$$

Solution:
To eliminate the variable $y$, we can multiply the first equation by $2$ and the second equation by $3$:

$$
\begin{align*}
4x + 6y &= 14 \\
12x - 6y &= 6 \\
\end{align*}
$$

Now, we can add the equations to eliminate $y$:

$$
\begin{align*}
(4x + 6y) + (12x - 6y) &= 14 + 6 \\
16x &= 20 \\
\end{align*}
$$

Solving for $x$, we find $x = \frac{20}{16} = \frac{5}{4}$.

Substituting the value of $x$ back into the first equation, we can solve for $y$:

$$
2\left(\frac{5}{4}\right) + 3y = 7 \\
\frac{5}{2} + 3y = 7 \\
3y = 7 - \frac{5}{2} \\
3y = \frac{9}{2} \\
y = \frac{9}{6} = \frac{3}{2}
$$

Therefore, the solution to the system of equations is $x = \frac{5}{4}$ and $y = \frac{3}{2}$.

Remember to always check your solution by substituting the values of $x$ and $y$ into both equations to ensure they satisfy the original system.

The directrix of a parabola is a fixed line that is perpendicular to the axis of symmetry. It is equidistant from every point on the parabola, but on the opposite side compared to the focus. The equation of the directrix can be written as y = k - p.

$$y = ax^2 + bx + c$$

### Section: Basic Theoretical Probability

In this section, we will introduce the concept of theoretical probability, which is the foundation of probability theory. Theoretical probability allows us to quantify the likelihood of an event occurring based on mathematical principles.

#### Definition and properties

Theoretical probability is defined as the ratio of the number of favorable outcomes to the total number of possible outcomes. It is denoted by the symbol $P(A)$, where $A$ represents the event of interest. The formula for calculating theoretical probability is:

$$
P(A) = \frac{{\text{{Number of favorable outcomes}}}}{{\text{{Total number of possible outcomes}}}}
$$

Let's consider an example to understand this concept better. Suppose we have a standard deck of 52 playing cards, and we want to find the probability of drawing a heart from the deck. The number of favorable outcomes in this case is 13 (as there are 13 hearts in a deck), and the total number of possible outcomes is 52 (as there are 52 cards in total). Therefore, the theoretical probability of drawing a heart is:

$$
P(\text{{heart}}) = \frac{{13}}{{52}} = \frac{{1}}{{4}}
$$

- $P(A)$ represents the probability of event A,

$$P(A \cap B) = 0$$
